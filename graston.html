How Graston Technique works

Graston Technique uses curved stainless steel tools to glide over tissue and locate rough or restricted areas. The tools help identify adhesions, stimulate local circulation, and support tissue remodeling.

  • The tool scans tissue and detects rough or restricted areas
  • It helps break up scar tissue and fascial adhesions
  • It stimulates blood flow and fibroblast activity
  • The goal is better mobility, tissue glide, and less stiffness
Core idea: scan + break + restore = improved mobility.

What Graston aftermath may look like

After Graston work, some people may notice redness, scattered petechiae, or darker discoloration depending on tissue restriction, circulation response, and sensitivity. These responses can vary from person to person.

  • Light redness may reflect fresh circulation and mild response
  • Petechiae are small red dots that can appear as tissues respond
  • Darker responses can suggest deeper, longer-standing restriction
  • These marks commonly fade as the area recovers
These skin responses are not automatically bruises. They are often part of the tissue response and healing process.
